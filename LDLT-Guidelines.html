1. Structure of an LDLT Edition

This section describes the basic structure of the file that contains an edition for the Digital Latin Library's Library of Digital Latin Texts (LDLT). The structure of an LDLT edition resembles the structure of a traditional printed critical edition:

Parts of an LDLT Edition
  • Publication Details
  • Front Matter
  • Critical Text (including apparatus)
  • Indices

Since every text is unique, not every LDLT edition will have all of these sections. The nature of the text and the editor's judgment will determine the contents of an edition. The purpose of this section is to define the parts of an LDLT edition and provide guidelines for representing them in XML.

These guidelines are based on the standard set by the Text Encoding Initiative (TEI), with modification and customization to accommodate the special characteristics of Latin texts.

1.1.

The following is an example of the general overall structure of an XML file for an LDLT edition:

1.2. Publication Details

A traditional critical edition in print has a title page announcing the title of the work, its author, the editor(s), and details of publication. In an LDLT edition, these details are presented as structured data within a TEI header (<teiHeader>). For example:

<teiHeader>  <fileDesc>   <titleStmt>    <title>Bucolica</title>    <author>Calpurnius Siculus</author>    <editor>Jane Doe</editor>   </titleStmt>   <editionStmt>    <edition>First Edition</edition>   </editionStmt>   <publicationStmt>    <publisher>Society for Classical Studies</publisher>    <distributor>Digital Latin Library</distributor>    <date>2016</date>    <availability>     <license target="https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/4.0/">Distributed under a Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International          License.</license>    </availability>   </publicationStmt>   <seriesStmt>    <title level="s">Library of Digital Latin Texts</title>    <biblScope unit="vol">1</biblScope>   </seriesStmt>   <sourceDesc>    <p>Born digital.</p>   </sourceDesc>  </fileDesc> </teiHeader>

Since a critical edition is often based upon many sources (e.g., manuscripts, previous editions, commentaries, etc.), the <teiHeader> for an LDLT edition does not hew strictly to the TEI guidelines for <sourceDesc>. The sources for an LDLT edition are documented and described in the edition's front matter.

Just as an editor is not expected to create the title page and document the publication information, this section of an LDLT is the responsibility of the DLL staff.

1.3. Front Matter

The <front> contains numbered divisions and subdivisions (e.g., <div1>, <div2>) corresponding to the preface, bibliography, and conspectus for the edition.

The preface lays out (in prose, using <p>) the argument for the edition. It also describes the textual tradition and the major sources for it, which is why it is recommended to compile the bibliography first, to take advantage of the ability to link to individual entries in the bibliography. For example, prefaces traditionally contain a description in prose of the manuscripts consulted in establishing the text of the edition. The preface of an LDLT can include prose descriptions of manuscripts, but it should also link to structured (i.e., machine-readable) data for the manuscript in the edition's bibliography.

The elements available in all TEI documents are available for use in the preface. For example, an editor might want to make a table comparing the readings of manuscript families. In that case, the editor would use <table> and its related elements.

1.4. Bibliography

The bibliography presents opportunities for leveraging the digital nature of an LDLT edition. Because every item in the bibliography is encoded as structured data (contained in <msDesc> or <biblStruct>), the bibliography itself can become a useful tool for finding and using resources, especially those that are available online.

The bibliography is all the more useful as a tool if it is created and made available via Zotero, an open, online resource for building and managing bibliographies. In addition to providing an interface for compiling and managing a bibliography, Zotero can also export bibliographies in a TEI XML format that requires little to no modification for inclusion in an LDLT edition. Users can copy and paste Zotero information directly into an LDLT edition file. For these reasons, it is highly recommended that all LDLT editions have a corresponding Zotero repository for their bibliography.

Unfortunately, Zotero does not currently provide a format for describing and cataloging manuscripts, so that information must be entered manually. For that reason, and for the sake of completeness, the following sections provide information for encoding items in the bibliography, with examples.

1.4.1. Structure of the Bibliography

The bibliography should be organized according to the following structure:

Structure of the Bibliography for an LDLT Edition
  • Manuscripts (xml:id="bibliography-manuscripts")
  • Editions
    • Early Editions (xml:id="bibliography-editions-witnesses"
    • Modern Editions (xml:id="bibliography-editions-sources")
  • Secondary Sources (xml:id="bibliography-secondary-sources")

Additionally, there should be a list of the names of individual scholars (xml:id="bibliography-scholars") referred to by name in the apparatus, if they are not listed elsewhere in the bibliography (e.g., as the editor of an edition or author of an article). It is common, for example, to refer to a scholar whose unpublished notes appear in a commentary or who has corresponded with the editor. For ease of reference, these sources should be listed with information about where the information can be verified.

1.4.2. Human- and Machine-Readable Sigla, Symbols, and Abbreviations

Printed editions have long used a system of shorthand references for manuscripts and other materials in the bibliography. Single letters, or sigla, stand for a manuscript, and the last name of a scholar serves as a shorthand reference to an edition, commentary, or other work of scholarship. Centuries of publication have demontrated that sigla and other symbols and abbreviations are efficient, effective, and economic ways of conveying complex information in critical editions. But they are also examples of visually encoded information that is available only to human readers. In other words, a human might see N² and correctly interpret it within a perticular edition as meaning "text written by the second person in a succession of people who wrote in the manuscript known as codex Neapolitanus V A 8." But a machine will interpret the same information as simply the letter N with a superscript numeral 2 next to it.

To make an LDLT text readable by both humans and machines, there must be a system of reference that leverages both the traditional approach that has served textual criticism so well for so long and new ways of making information available to machines for processing and sophisticated analysis. To accomplish that goal, each item in the bibliography of an LDLT edition must have two unique identifiers: one for humans and one for machines.

For the latter, the XML specification has the xml:id attribute, which has strict rules that do not allow for the traditional conventions used in critical editions. For the former, the TEI standard has n, which allows for all of the usual conventions. In same instances, the two attributes might be identical. For example, if a manuscript is known by the siglum V, it is possible to identify it to machines as xml:id="V" and to humans as n="V". But if an editor must refer to a number of manuscripts from the same collection designated as V, the usual practice in a printed critical edition is to use the letter V to stand for the collection with subscript numerals distinguishing the different manuscripts. In an LDLT edition, the second one in the series would have xml:id of "V2", but the n would be V₂. Humans can read and understand both easily, but machines need a simpler method of identifying something.

Every entry, regardless of type, in the bibliography of an LDLT edition must have values for both xml:id and n. Otherwise, the edition will not be able to make full use of the digital format. Morever, if an entry lacks n, it will not be available for the print version of the edition.

1.4.3. Manuscripts

Manuscript description is a discipline unto itself, with an entire module in the TEI guidelines devoted to it (10 Manuscript Description). Since fully describing every manuscript consulted for an edition could be prohibitive, these guidelines set forth the minimum required for the LDLT.

<listWit> (list of witnesses) contains the descriptions of manuscript sources for the edition.

<witness> (witness) groups the information about a manuscript, including the name used to refer to the manuscript in the edition (e.g., Codex Neapolitanus V A 8). Its xml:id is the unique machine-readable identifier to be used when referring to the manuscript's data (e.g., in the wit of a <rdg> in the apparatus criticus).

<msDesc> (manuscript description) groups the elements that compose the description of the manuscript:

Required Elements in the Manuscript Description
  • <msIdentifier> (manuscript identifier) groups information about the location and inventory number of the manuscript.
  • <settlement> contains the name of the place (e.g., city) where the manuscript is located.
  • <institution> contains the name of the library that houses the manuscript
  • <repository> contains the name of the collection to which the manuscript belongs.
  • <idno> (identifier) contains the catalog or inventory information for the manuscript.
  • <altIdentifier> (alternative identifier) contains other information (i.e., in addition to the <idno>) used to identify the manuscript.
  • <phsyDesc> (physical description) groups information about the manuscript.
  • <objectDesc> (object description) contains <supportDesc> (support description). It may have form to identify whether the manuscript is a codex, a set of unbound leaves, or other form.
  • <supportDesc> (support description) is a self-closing element that identifies (in material) the material upon which the manuscript's text is written (e.g., "parchment").
  • <handDesc> (hand description) groups information about the distinct hands that wrote in the manuscript. Use hands with the value of the number of hands detectable in the manuscript.
  • <handNote> is a self-closing element with attributes to distinguish one hand from another. Each <handNote> has an xml:id with a unique identifier (based on the xml:id of the manuscript), an n with a human-readable version of the value of the hand's xml:id, scope with value "major" or "minor" to indicate the extent of the hand's contribution, and, if necessary, corresp with the value of the xml:id for any discussion of the hand's contribution in the preface or elsewhere in the addition. Note that the first hand should also have sameAs with the value of the manuscript's xml:id, so that references to both N and N¹ will point to the same entity, that is, the original copyist.
  • <history> groups information about the date of the manuscript.
  • <origin> encloses <origDate>, which contains the Roman numeral for the century in which the manuscript was written, or the Arabic numeral for the year, if known. If century is used, include notBefore and notAfter with appropriate values. If year is used, enclose it with <date>.
<listWit>  <head>First family</head>  <witness n="N" xml:id="N">Codex Neapolitanus V A 8 <msDesc>    <msIdentifier>     <settlement>Naples</settlement>     <repository>Biblioteca Nazionale di Napoli</repository>     <idno>V A 8</idno>     <altIdentifier>      <idno>380</idno>     </altIdentifier>    </msIdentifier>    <physDesc>     <objectDesc form="codex">      <supportDesc material="parch"/>     </objectDesc>     <handDesc hands="3">      <handNote corresp="#Ndesc-manusprima"       n="N¹" sameAs="#N" scope="major" xml:id="N1"/>      <handNote corresp="#Ndesc-manusaltera"       n="N²" scope="major" xml:id="N2"/>      <handNote corresp="#Ndesc-manustertia"       n="N³" scope="minor" xml:id="N3"/>     </handDesc>    </physDesc>    <history>     <origin>      <origDate notAfter="1500"       notBefore="1400">XV</origDate>     </origin>    </history>   </msDesc>  </witness>  <witness n="G" xml:id="G">Codex Gaddianus pl. 90, 12 inf. <msDesc>    <msIdentifier>     <settlement>Florence</settlement>     <repository>Biblioteca Medicea Laurenziana</repository>     <idno>plut. 90, 12 inf.</idno>    </msIdentifier>    <physDesc>     <objectDesc form="codex">      <supportDesc material="paper"/>     </objectDesc>     <handDesc hands="2">      <handNote n="G¹" sameAs="#G"       xml:id="G1"/>      <handNote n="G²" xml:id="G2"/>     </handDesc>    </physDesc>    <history>     <origin>      <origDate notAfter="1500"       notBefore="1400" xml:id="witGdate">XV</origDate>      <note target="#witGdate">saec. XV ineunte scriptus</note>     </origin>    </history>   </msDesc>  </witness> </listWit>

Note that <note> may be used to specify the portion of the century in which a manuscript was written, if known.

If it is important to distinguish among families of manuscripts, separate <listWit> containers (each with an identifying <head>) should be used. In cases of multiple <listWit> containers, each <listWit> should have a unique xml:id to use when referring to the family itself or a consensus of readings in that family.

1.4.4. Other Sources

1.4.4.1. Common Elements for All Entries

All entries, regardless of the type, will have certain common elements.

1.4.4.1.1. <biblStruct>

Entries for items other than manuscripts should be contained in <biblStruct> with xml:id and n as described above. Entries should also be classified with type and encoded according to the guidelines and examples below.

1.4.4.1.2. Publication Information

Publication information for all types of entries should be handled with these elements:

<imprint> groups information pertaining to the publication of the edition

<pubPlace> (place of publication) contains the name of the place where the edition was published, as it appears on the title page (e.g., Lipsiae instead of Leipzig). If the place is unknown, <pubPlace> should be self-closing (e.g., <pubPlace/>).

<publisher> contains the name of the publisher, as it appears on the title page. If the publisher's name is unknown, <publisher> should be self-closing (e.g., <publisher/>).

<date> contains the date in Arabic numerals (e.g., 1504, not MDIV). If the date is unknown, <date> should be self-closing (e.g., <date/>).

<note> with type="url" contains the URL for an online copy of the edition, if available.

1.4.4.2. Editions

Previous editions must be classified in one of two categories: early editions based on a single manuscript and modern critical editions based on more than one source. This will have ramifications for how the editions are handled in the apparatus criticus (see below, Apparatus Criticus). This distinction should be preserved in the bibliography with the use of two <listBibl> lists, with type "witness" and "source", respectively. The structure of the individual entries should be the same in both lists.

The individual entries in each <listBibl> should have type="book".

<biblStruct> (structured bibliographic citation) is the top-level element for each edition. It should have type of "book". The values for xml:id and n should be based on the surname of the editor. Additionally, if the bibliography has been generated by Zotero, each entry should have corresp with the URL for the entry in the online Zotero bibliography.

<monogr> (monographic level) contains bibliographic elements describing an edition.

<title> with level="m" (monographic) contains the full title of the edition.

<editor> contains information identifying the editor of the edition. If the edition has more than one editor, use one <editor> for each editor.

<name> contains the value "Anonymous" in cases where the name of the editor is not known.

<forename> contains the first and middle names, or initials, of an editor in cases where that information is known.

<surname> contains the last name of an editor, in cases where that information is known. If the editor is known by a nickname or a Latin name, that information is enclosed in parentheses and quotation marks: ("Ascensius").

Publication information is handled according to the guidelines in the section above on publication.

<biblStruct corresp="http://zotero.org/groups/542622/items/UAZU56E4"  n="Nordh." type="book" xml:id="Nordh.">  <monogr>   <title level="m">[Bucolica]</title>   <editor>    <name>Anonymous</name>   </editor>   <imprint>    <pubPlace>Nordheim</pubPlace>    <publisher/>    <date/>   </imprint>  </monogr> </biblStruct> <biblStruct corresp="http://zotero.org/groups/542622/items/TXA4M9HA"  n="s" type="book" xml:id="s">  <monogr>   <title level="m">Buccolica, cum adnotatione Ascensiana, jam      diligentius recognita</title>   <editor xml:id="Ascensius">    <forename>Josse</forename>    <surname>Badius ("Ascensius")</surname>   </editor>   <imprint>    <pubPlace>Paris</pubPlace>    <publisher>In vico Maturinorum a Durando Gerlerio</publisher>    <date>1503</date>    <note target="#s" type="url">http://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/bpt6k841539v</note>   </imprint>  </monogr> </biblStruct>
1.4.4.3. Books Other than Editions

The bibliographical information for monographs, commentaries, and other books that are not editions should be handled using these elements and attributes:

<biblStruct> (structured bibliographic citation) is the top-level element for each book. It should have type of "book". The values for xml:id and n should be based on the surname of the author. Additionally, if the bibliography has been generated by Zotero, each entry should have corresp with the URL for the entry in the online Zotero bibliography.

<monogr> (monographic level) groups bibliographic elements describing a book.

<title> with level="m" (monographic) contains the full title of the book.

<author> contains information identifying the author of the book. If the book has more than one author, use one <author> for each author.

<name> contains the value "Anonymous" in cases where the name of the author is not known.

<forename> contains the first and middle names (middle name optional), or initials, of an author in cases where that information is known.

<surname> contains the last name of an author, in cases where that information is known.

Publication information is handled according to the guidelines in the section above on publication.

1.4.4.4. Articles

Bibliographic details for articles published in journals should be encoded using the following elements and attributes:

<biblStruct> (structured bibliographic citation) is the top-level element for each article. It should have type of "journalArticle" (note case). The values for xml:id and n should be based on the surname of the author. Additionally, if the bibliography has been generated by Zotero, each entry should have corresp with the URL for the entry in the online Zotero bibliography.

<analytic> (analytic level) contains items relating to an article published within a journal volume.

Items Contained By <analytic>
  • <title> with level="a" contains the title of the article.
  • <author> contains information identifying the author of the book. If the book has more than one author, use one <author> for each author.
  • <forename> contains the first and middle names (middle name optional), or initials, of an author in cases where that information is known.
  • <surname> contains the last name of an author, in cases where that information is known.

<monogr> (monographic level) groups bibliographic elements describing the journal.

Items Contained by <mongr>
  • <title> with level="j" contains the title of the journal.
  • <imprint> groups several <biblScope> elements defining publication information. All numbers should be Arabic numerals.
  • <biblScope> (scope of bibliographic reference) is used with unit to indicate details of publication:
    • unit="volume" contains the volume number of the journal in which the article has been published
    • unit="issue" contains the number of the issue within the volume
    • unit="page" contains the inclusive pages where the article may be found
  • <date> contains the year of publication
  • <note> with type="url" contains the URL where the article may be read online, if available.

Note that a review of an edition or other work should be handled in the same way as a journal article, with the exception of the <title> in <analytic>, which should begin with the words "Review of" followed by the title of the work being reviewed.

1.4.4.5. List of Scholars

Occasionally it is necessary to refer to a scholar whose unpublished work has been quoted or paraphrased elsewhere. For example, editors often cite Nicolaas Heinsius' unpublished conjectures, found in books in his own library. But "Heins." does not help a reader find more information. That is why the bibliography of an LDLT edition includes a list of scholars mentioned by name in the edition, with information about where to find their remarks, if they are available.

<list> with xml:id="bibliography-scholars" groups the information identifying the scholars.

<item> with xml:id and n as described above.

<persName> (personal name) groups the information about the scholar's name. Note that it is helpful, but not required, to use ref with the Virtual International Authorify File (VIAF) permalink for that person.

<forename> contains the scholar's first and middle names (middle name optional), or initials, of the scholar.

<surname> contains the scholar's last name. If the scholars is known by a nickname or shortened name (e.g., Wilamowitz), that information is enclosed in parentheses and quotation marks.

<ref> (reference) contains a reference to the entry in the bibliography that has citations of the scholar. target with the xml:id of the entry will cause the reference to point to that entry.

<note> contains further information about the nature of the citation (e.g., "personal correspondence with editor").

<item n="C. Schenkl" xml:id="CSchenkl">  <persName>   <forename>Karl</forename>   <surname>Schenkl</surname>  </persName>  <ref target="#Schenkl">H. Schenkl</ref>  <note>Unpublished opinions cited in Schenkl's editions.</note> </item> <item n="Scriver." xml:id="Scriverius">  <persName ref="https://viaf.org/viaf/121894458/">   <forename>Pieter</forename>   <surname>Schrijver ("Scriverius")</surname>  </persName>  <ref target="#Burm.">Burman 1731</ref> </item>

2. Edition Text

The edition text corresponds to the text printed "above the line" of the critical apparatus in a traditional edition. It is composed of "accepted readings," that is, text judged by the editor to be authoritative.

There may be more than one authoritative version of a text, depending on the text and depending on the approach adopted by the editor. Authors sometimes edit and revise their work over time. Scribes often record the version of the text that makes sense in their context. Scholars and other readers offer conjectures for problematic passages. But an editor must make decisions about the text and edition presents to readers. The results of those decisions are on display in the official edition text.

Since the online version of an LDLT allows readers to swap readings from the critical apparatus into the main text, it is possible to encounter many versions of the text in one viewing of it, but the official edition text is the starting point for those interactions.

This section establishes guidelines for encoding the information typically found in the main text of a critical edition.

2.1. Prose

2.2. Verse

The verse module of the TEI Guidelines offers many possibilities for marking up various aspects of prosody, but the LDLT is primarily concerned with the core tags for encoding the structure of verse texts. This does not mean that editors and others should avoid using the verse module. It means only that the LDLT does not provide functionality for that level of detail.

<div1> (level-1 text division) groups the parts of a book of verse, with type="book" and n with the number of the book.

<div2> (level-2 text division) groups the parts of a single poem (e.g., stanzas or lines), with type="poem", n with the number of the poem in the book, and met with the name of the meter.

<lg> (line group) groups one or more verse lines that form a stanza or a couplet, with type and "stanza" or "couplet".

<l> (line) contains the text of a single line of verse, with n and the number of the line.

<div1 n="2" type="book">  <div2 met="Alcaic Strophe" n="1"   type="poem">   <lg type="stanza">    <l n="1">Motum ex Metello consule civicum</l>    <l n="2">bellique causas et vitia et modos</l>    <l n="3">ludumque Fortunae gravisque</l>    <l n="4">principum amicitias et arma</l>   </lg>  </div2> </div1>

2.5. Symbols Commonly Used in the Text of a Critical Edition

The symbols in this section have been gleaned from Martin West, Textual Criticism and Editorial Technique (Stuttgart, 1973), 80–81.

2.5.1. Abbreviation Fully Expanded

<expan> (expansion) contains the full expansion of an abbreviation.

<abbr> (abbreviation) contains the abbreviated part, i.e. the letters actually on the page.

<ex> (editorial expansion) contains a sequence of letters added by an editor or transcriber when expanding an abbreviation.

<supplied> signifies text supplied by the transcriber or editor.

‘a(bc)’

<expan>  <abbr>a</abbr>  <ex>bc</ex> </expan>

Multiple <abbr> elements within a single <expan> compose a single abbreviation between them. For example, e.g. is one abbreviation for the phrase "exempli gratia."

<expan>  <abbr>e</abbr>  <ex>xempli</ex>  <abbr>g</abbr>  <ex>ratia</ex> </expan>

2.5.2. Text added by emendation

An editor supplies text believed to be missing from the original source.

<supplied> signifies text believed by the transcriber or editor to be missing.

reason indicates that text has been lost, but supplied by emendation.

‘<abc>’

<app>  <lem source="#Vettel">   <supplied reason="lost">abc</supplied>  </lem> </app>

2.5.3. Undetermined Amount Of Text Lost Through Damage

Text has been lost from one or more witnesses because of damage.

<gap> signifies text missing because of damage.

reason with value "lost" indicates that the text has been lost.

extent with value "unknown" indicates that an undetermined amount of text has been lost.

unit with value "characters" indicates that an unspecified number of characters (whether single characters or words or larger groups of text) have been lost.

‘[ ]’

<gap extent="unknown" reason="lost"  unit="characters"/>

2.5.4. Known Amount Of Text Lost Through Damage

The editor or transcriber knows from context and/or observation how much text has been lost.

<gap> signifies text missing because of damage.

reason with value "lost" indicates that the text has been lost.

quantity indicates the amount of text has been lost.

unit indicates the unit measured in quantity.

‘[ ... ]’

<gap quantity="3" reason="lost"  unit="characters"/>

2.5.5. Editorial Deletion

Text suspected by the editor to be an interpolation.

<surplus> signifies text identified by the editor as added by someone other than the original author.

reason with value "interpolation" indicates why the text has been marked as surplus.

‘{lorem ipsum}’

<app>  <lem source="#Alonso">   <surplus reason="interpolation">lorem ipsum</surplus>  </lem> </app>

2.5.6. Deleted But Legible Text

Text deleted in an ancient source but still legible (more or less clearly).

<del> indicates the text that has been deleted but is still legible.

rend with value "erasure" indicates that the text has been erased. Other values: "strikethrough" and "expunction".

‘[[co]]gaudete’

<del rend="erasure">co</del>gaudete

2.5.7. Deleted and Lost

Text deleted in an ancient source to the point of illegibility.

<del> indicates that a deletion has occurred.

rend indicates the method of deletion (e.g., erasure, expunction, strikethrough).

<gap> indicates that the deletion has left a gap of irrecoverable text.

reason with value "lost" indicates that the text is irrecoverable.

quantity indicates the extent of the deletion.

unit indicates the unit of measurement for quantityt.

‘[[ ... ]]’

<del rend="erasure">  <gap quantity="3" unit="characters"/> </del>

2.5.8. Deleted and Illegible But Restored

Text deleted in an ancient source to the point of illegibility, but restored by the editor (e.g., by comparison with another exemplar).

<del> indicates that a deletion has occurred.

rend indicates the method of deletion (e.g., erasure, expunction).

<supplied> indicates the text that has been restored.

reason with value "lost" indicates why the text had to be supplied.

<orig> indicates that the supplied original cannot be understood.

<unclear> indicates that the supplied original is unclear.

‘[[ abc ]] ’

Text deemed by the editor to be irredeemably corrupt.

<unclear> indicates that the text is readable but not understandable.

3. Apparatus Criticus

This section provides models for handling the different kinds of data that occur in the apparatus criticus.

<app> (apparatus entry) contains the lemma and readings for a single entry in the apparatus criticus, though it and its contents may be nested inside of another <app>

<lem> (lemma) contains a reading judged by the editor to be what the author wrote, or as close as possible. This type of reading constitutes the text printed in the main portion of a critical edition.

<rdg> (reading) contains some text (or absence of text) witnessed by a manuscript or found in an early printed edition where it is not expressly proposed, but judged by the editor to be unsatisfactory or corrupt for linguistic and/or stylistic reasons. This text is printed in the apparatus criticus.

wit (witness) indicates any manuscript(s) or early printed edition(s) (esp. without an apparatus or commentary; i.e., editiones vetustiores)

source (source) indicates any printed edition(s) or other scholarly work(s) (e.g., article, textual note, commentary) addressing an aspect of the text.

ana (analysis) indicates that a reading belongs to at least one category in the LDLT's taxonomy of readings. More than one value is possible.

<witDetail> (witness detail) contains information about a particular witness (wit) to a lemma (<lem>) or reading (<rdg>).

<note> (note) contains editorial comments, bibliographical references, or other information pertaining to the apparatus entry.

3.1.

Need discussion about the order in which information is presented.

3.2. Basic reporting of a variant reading

A witness or source has a reading that varies from the lemma in some way. For example, if the editor has accepted the text ‘Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet’ into the main text of the edition, but manuscript A has ‘larem’ instead of ‘lorem’, the variant reading would be reported in a printed edition as follows:

‘18 larem A’

Since the lemma is not typically printed in an apparatus criticus (unless there is a chance for confusion), it is up to the reader to recognize that ‘larem’ is a variant for ‘lorem’.

In this model, an <app> contains both the lemma and the variant reading, reducing the need for guesswork, at least when looking at the XML code.

The patterns displayed below are designed to handle the various kinds of information conveyed in a typical apparatus criticus, including readings ante correctionem and in rasura, among other paleographical and editorial details.

<p>  <app>   <lem>Lorem</lem>   <rdg wit="#A">larem</rdg>  </app> ipsum dolor sit amet </p>

If an editor deems it important to indicate that a <rdg> also appears in a source, the same pattern applies, but source is added after wit:

<p>  <app>   <lem>Lorem</lem>   <rdg source="#Jones" wit="#A">larem</rdg>  </app> ipsum dolor sit amet </p>

If there are multiple variant readings, another <rdg> should be added for each one.

<p>  <app>   <lem>Lorem</lem>   <rdg source="#Jones" wit="#A">larem</rdg>   <rdg wit="#B">lurum</rdg>  </app> ipsum dolor sit amet </p>

3.3. Additions

A witness or source adds text (e.g., in the margin) that an editor deems worthy of notice.

<add> (addition) contains text that has been added.

place indicates the placed where the text has been added. Possible values:

  • above: above the line (suprascr. = superscriptum/a)
  • below: below the line (subscr. = subscriptum/a)
  • bottom: bottom of the page (ad fin. = ad finem
  • inline: within the body of the original text (in textu)
  • inRas: in the space where text has been erased. (in ras. = in rasura)
  • margin: right, left, or both (in mg. = in margine)
  • top: at the top of the page (ad init. = ad initium

hand indicates the manuscript hand that added the text. See the section on manuscript hands for more information about encoding that information.

For example, the copyist of manuscript N has the lemma, but a second hand has added a variant reading in the margin.

‘89 certare] certate G in mg.’

<app>  <lem>certare</lem>  <rdg wit="#G" xml:id="rdg89certate">   <add hand="#G1" place="margin">certate</add>  </rdg> </app>

3.4. Deletions

A witness or source has deleted some text, and an editor deems it worthy of notice.

<del> (deletion) contains text that has been deleted.

rend (rendition) indicates the method of deletion. Possible values:

  • erasure: text has been physically removed from the page or lines have been drawn through it (del. = delevit/deleverunt)
  • expunction: text has been marked for deletion with dots underneath (exp. = expunxit/expunxerunt)

For example, the copyist of manuscript N has printed the lemma tristique, but a later hand has deleted the letters que.

‘tristi (que del. N²)’

<app ana="#subtractive">  <lem>tristique</lem>  <rdg wit="#N">tristi<del hand="#N2" rend="erasure">que</del>  </rdg> </app>

Note that ana with value "subtractive" is on the <app> to indicate that the entire entry concerns a reading that takes away something from the lemma. If there are several <rdg> elements and not all of them are subtractive in nature, ana would be applied to the relevant <rdg>.

3.5. Omissions

A witness or source omits some text and the editor deems it important to report the omission.

For example, the witness known as "P" has omitted the word pecus from the following text: securo custode pecus nocturnaque pastor. The traditional apparatus criticus indicates the omission as follows:

‘pecus om. P’

In this model, the omission is represented by a "self-closing" <rdg>:

<l>securo custode <app ana="#subtractive">   <lem>pecus</lem>   <rdg wit="#P"/>  </app> nocturnaque pastor</l>

Note that ana is used with the value "subtractive" to enable querying for this type of data. If there is only one <rdg>, then ana="subtractive" is attached to <app> to indicate that the entire entry in the apparatus concerns an omission. If an omission is to be reported among other readings, ana="subtractive" is applied to the <rdg> that reports the omission:

<l>securo custode <app>   <lem>pecus</lem>   <rdg wit="#B">pecore</rdg>   <rdg ana="#subtractive" wit="#P"/>  </app> nocturnaque pastor</l>

The same model applies to the omission of an entire line of verse, but in that case <app> (with type="lineOmission") and <lem> contain the entire line, and the omission is marked by a self-closing <rdg>:

<app type="lineOmission">  <lem>   <l n="3">et spument rauco ferventia musta susurro.</l>  </lem>  <rdg wit="#κ #χ"/> </app>

This is displayed in the apparatus criticus as ‘vers. om. κχ’

3.6. Lacuna

A witness is missing a portion of the text.

<lacunaStart> is a self-closing tag with wit indicating where in a witness the lacuna begins.

<lacunaEnd> is a self-closing tag with wit indicating where in a witness the lacuna ends.

For example, witness N has a lacuna that begins after the word metus and ends before the word arcu in the following passage: ‘Mauris ut mauris et metus pharetra dictum et risus. Suspendisse ut maximus diam. Suspendisse luctus diam non libero pellentesque, et aliquam ex congue. Suspendisse vitae lacinia urna, nec egestas arcu. Quisque eget.’

A traditional apparatus criticus might have the following to indicate the lacuna:

‘pharetra … arcu deest N’

<p>Mauris ut mauris et metus <app>   <lem>pharetra</lem>   <rdg wit="#N">    <lacunaStart/>   </rdg>  </app> dictum et risus. Suspendisse ut maximus diam. Suspendisse luctus diam non libero pellentesque, et aliquam ex congue. Suspendisse vitae lacinia urna, nec <app>   <lem>egestas</lem>   <rdg wit="#N">    <lacunaEnd/>   </rdg>  </app> arcu. Quisque eget.</p>

3.7. Corrections

A variant reading has been corrected to the value of the lemma, but the editor deems the reading prior to correction worthy of notice.

<rdgGrp> (reading group) contains the readings before and after correction, but since the emphasis is on the original, uncorrected, reading, type is applied with the value "anteCorr" (i.e., ante correctionem)

varSeq (variant sequence) indicates the order of the readings (e.g., 1 for the first reading, 2 for the correction.

copyOf indicates that the correction is a copy of the lemma. Instead of copying the value of the lemma, we point to it to indicate that they are the same.

For example, manuscript N originally had quaedam for the lemma quidem. The editor wishes to note the reading ante correctionem.

In a traditional critical edition, this would be handled in the apparatus criticus as follows: ‘quaedam N (ante corr. N¹).’ There is no need to print the result of the correction, since "correction" implies the editor's accepted reading (i.e., lemma).

<app>  <lem xml:id="lem1.33quidem">quidem</lem>  <rdgGrp type="anteCorr">   <rdg varSeq="1" wit="#N">quaedam</rdg>   <rdg copyOf="#lem1.33quidem" varSeq="2"    wit="#N1"/>  </rdgGrp> </app>

Note that the <lem> must have an xml:id to be a target for copyOf

This pattern is the basis for a number of other representations of reading sequences.

3.7.1. Correction in the Margin

A manuscript hand has corrected a variant reading to the lemma by writing it in the margin.

For example, manuscript G has iantabimur in the text, but a second hand has added the accepted reading mactabimus in the margin. This would be handled in a traditional apparatus as follows: ‘iantabimur G (corr. G² in mg.)’.

<app>  <lem xml:id="lem4.166mactabimus">mactabimus</lem>  <rdgGrp type="anteCorr">   <rdg varSeq="1" wit="#G">iantabimur</rdg>   <rdg varSeq="2" wit="#G">    <add copyOf="#lem4.166mactabimus"     place="margin"/>   </rdg>  </rdgGrp> </app>

A self-closing <add> is used here with place and copyOf to indicate that the marginal reading is identical to the accepted reading.

3.7.2. Correction of an Omission

The original copyist has omitted a reading from the text, but another hand (or the original copyist) has supplied the missing text.

For example, manuscript G has omitted the lemma tandem, but the original copyist has supplied it. In a traditional critical edition, that would be represented as follows: ‘tandem] om. G (corr. G¹)’.

<app>  <lem xml:id="lem1.43tandem">tandem</lem>  <rdgGrp type="corrOmission">   <rdg varSeq="1" wit="#G"/>   <rdg copyOf="#lem1.43tandem" varSeq="2"    wit="#G1"/>  </rdgGrp> </app>

If the corrector has added the reading in the margin or elsewhere (e.g., ‘tandem] om. G (corr. G¹ in mg.)’, <add> with place and copyOf can be used:

<app ana="#subtractive">  <lem xml:id="lem1.43tandem">tandem</lem>  <rdgGrp type="corrOmission">   <rdg varSeq="1" wit="#G"/>   <rdg varSeq="2" wit="#G">    <add copyOf="#lem1.43tandem"     place="margin"/>   </rdg>  </rdgGrp> </app>

3.7.3. Correction by Addition

A variant reading differs from the lemma by one or more characters, which have been supplied by the original copyist or another hand at a later time.

For example, manuscript N has insidia, which differs from the lemma insidiaby a single letter "s". A later hand has added the missing "s". That might be handled as follows in a traditional apparatus criticus: insidia N (s add. N²).

<app>  <lem xml:id="lem3.2insidias">insidias</lem>  <rdgGrp type="anteCorr">   <rdg varSeq="1" wit="#N">insidia</rdg>   <rdg varSeq="2" wit="#N2">insidia<add place="inline">s</add>   </rdg> <!-- Or should this be handled by a <witDetail>? Or both? -->  </rdgGrp> </app>

3.7.4. Correction by Deletion

A variant reading differs from the lemma by one or more characters, which have been deleted by the original copyist or another hand at a later time.

For example, manuscript N has the reading cogaudete where the lemma is just gaudete. The original copyist has deleted the letters "co" from the beginning of the word. In a traditional apparatus criticus, that might be printed as follows: ‘cogaudete N (corr. N¹, co exp.)’.

<app>  <lem xml:id="lem2.5gaudete">gaudete</lem>  <rdgGrp type="anteCorr">   <rdg varSeq="1" wit="#N">cogaudete</rdg>   <rdg varSeq="2" wit="#N1"    xml:id="rdg2.5gaudete">    <del rend="expunction">co</del>gaudete</rdg>  </rdgGrp> </app>

3.7.5. One Witness Among Many Has Been Corrected

A variant reading has several witnesses, but only one of them has been corrected, and the editor believes it is important to make that clear.

For example, manuscripts P Q R S T U V have the variant reading flore where the lemma is Flora. Only manuscript T has been corrected. In a traditional apparatus criticus, that might be handled as follows: ‘flore PQRST (corr. T¹) UV’.

Since only one witness has been corrected, <rdgGrp> with type="anteCorr" cannot contain the reading of all of the manuscripts, so we must use copyOf on both the lemma and the variant reading.

<app>  <lem xml:id="lem1.2Flora">Flora</lem>  <rdg wit="#P #Q #R #S #U #V"   xml:id="rdg1.2">flore</rdg>  <rdgGrp>   <rdg copyOf="#rdg1.2flore" varSeq="1"    wit="#T"/>   <rdg copyOf="#lem1.2Flora" varSeq="2"    wit="#T1"/>  </rdgGrp> <!-- Need to consider whether this should be done with <witDetail> instead or in addition to it. --> </app>

3.7.6. Mutation

Sometimes the copyist or a later hand "corrects" a variant reading to another variant reading. Although the motivation may have been to correct, the action results in mutation.

For example, manuscript N has dipicta for lemma descripta, and the original copyist has "corrected" the reading to depicta.

A traditional apparatus criticus might handle that as follows: ‘dipicta N (mut. in depicta N¹)’.

The same pattern for the ante correctionem scenarios can be used here, with a different value for type ("mutation") on <rdgGrp>:

<app>  <lem wit="#P #V #N2">descripta</lem>  <rdgGrp type="mutation">   <rdg varSeq="1" wit="#N">dipicta</rdg>   <rdg varSeq="2" wit="#N1">depicta</rdg>  </rdgGrp> </app>

3.8. Conjecture

A conjecture is some portion of text (or absence of text) proposed by some person (ancient or modern) and determined by an editor to be worthy of consideration in establishing the text, but not to be printed in the main text of the edition.

In a traditional critical edition, a conjecture might be noted as follows: ‘rapidoque] rabidoque conj. Baehrens’.

Although conjectures are more common in modern editions and other scholarship, they are not unheard of in manuscripts, where a hand has written a reading (e.g., in the margin) that does not resemble any other variant readings and cannot be attributed to a recognizable error. Therefore, conjecture is not limited to those materials identified in source. For this reason, type with value "conjecture" must be used to identify a reading as a conjecture.

<app type="conjecture">  <lem>rapidoque</lem>  <rdg source="#Baehrens">rabidoque</rdg> </app>

In this example, type is attached to <app> because the entire entry concerns a conjecture. If the conjecture is just one among many readings, type must be used on the individual <rdg> that contains the conjecture.

<app>  <lem>bullantes</lem>  <rdg wit="#ε #δ #e #d2">bullentes</rdg>  <rdg wit="#η #ν #φ #ρ2 #r #s">bullantis</rdg>  <rdg source="#Heins." type="#conjecture">palantes</rdg> </app>

If a single scholar has proposed multiple conjectures for a single lemma, <rdgGrp> with type contains all of the conjectures as individual readings:

<app>  <lem>nullos ducet</lem>  <rdgGrp type="conjecture">   <rdg source="#Heins.">nullosque sui</rdg>   <rdg source="#Heins.">nullosque ducis</rdg>   <rdg source="#Heins.">nullosque ducum</rdg>  </rdgGrp> </app>

That should be displayed as: ‘nullosque sui vel nullosque duics vel nullosque ducum conj. Heins.’

3.9. Emendation

An emendation is a "successful" conjecture. That is, an emendation is some portion of text (or absence of text) expressly proposed by some person through the application of critical judgment, accepted by the editor in place of the transmitted readings, and printed in the main text of an edition. For this reason, only a lemma can be classified as an emendation.

The following example illustrates the difference between an emendation and a conjecture by virtue of the fact that the <lem> has type="emendation" and <rdg> has type="conjecture":

<app>  <lem source="#Ulit." type="emendation">niteat</lem>  <rdg wit="#N #G #β #κ #π #ρ1 #λ1">mutat</rdg>  <rdg source="#p #t #h #Kempfer #Burm."   wit="#P #δ">mittat</rdg>  <rdg ana="#lexical" wit="#μ">micat</rdg>  <rdg source="#o #n #l2 #i2 #Guid. #Wernsd. #Beck #Glaeser #Keene"   wit="#θ #ν #α #η #r #s">nutet</rdg>  <rdg source="#i1 #l1 #Tig. #Vienn."   wit="#ε #χ #γ #ρ2 #λ2 #d #e #u #b">nutat</rdg>  <rdg wit="#φ">mutet</rdg>  <rdg source="#Leo" type="conjecture">micet ac</rdg>  <rdg source="#Barth" type="conjecture">nictat</rdg> </app>

3.10. Notes

An editor wishes to comment on a reading, include bibliographical information, or note something else about the passage in question.

For example, the editor has recorded a variant reading that another scholar has supported in a textual note. That would be handled in a traditional edition as follows: molle sub] molliter Glaeser, def. Jacoby (Wochenschrift für klassische Philologie III, 1886, p. 1290).

We handle this with <note> with target pointing at the value of xml:id on the <lem> or <rdg> in question.

<app>  <lem source="#edd." wit="#P #V">molle sub</lem>  <rdg source="#Glaeser #Keene"   wit="#N #G #A #H" xml:id="rdg1.5molliter">molliter</rdg>  <rdg source="#Schenkl2">molle per</rdg>  <note target="#rdg1.5molliter">   <ref target="#Jacoby">Jacoby</ref> defendit    (<ref target="https://books.google.de/books?id=q5blAAAAMAAJ&amp;hl=de&amp;pg=PA128#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false">Wochenschrift für klassische Philologie</ref> III, 1886, p. 1290)</note> </app>
