1. Representing symbols commonly used in the main text of a critical edition

The symbols in this section have been gleaned from Martin West, Textual Criticism and Editorial Technique (Stuttgart, 1973), 80–81.

1.1. Abbreviation Fully Expanded

<expan> (expansion) contains the full expansion of an abbreviation.

<abbr> (abbreviation) contains the abbreviated part, i.e. the letters actually on the page.

<ex> (editorial expansion) contains a sequence of letters added by an editor or transcriber when expanding an abbreviation.

<supplied> signifies text supplied by the transcriber or editor.

‘a(bc)’

<expan>  <abbr>a</abbr>  <ex>bc</ex> </expan>

Multiple <abbr> elements within a single <expan> compose a single abbreviation between them. For example, e.g. is one abbreviation for the phrase "exempli gratia."

<expan>  <abbr>e</abbr>  <ex>xempli</ex>  <abbr>g</abbr>  <ex>ratia</ex> </expan>

1.2. Text added by emendation

An editor supplies text believed to be missing from the original source.

<supplied> signifies text believed by the transcriber or editor to be missing.

reason indicates that text has been lost, but supplied by emendation.

‘<abc>’

<app>  <lem source="#Vettel">   <supplied reason="lost">abc</supplied>  </lem> </app>

1.3. Undetermined Amount Of Text Lost Through Damage

Text has been lost from one or more witnesses because of damage.

<gap> signifies text missing because of damage.

reason with value "lost" indicates that the text has been lost.

extent with value "unknown" indicates that an undetermined amount of text has been lost.

unit with value "characters" indicates that an unspecified number of characters (whether single characters or words or larger groups of text) have been lost.

‘[ ]’

<gap extent="unknown" reason="lost"  unit="characters"/>

1.4. Known Amount Of Text Lost Through Damage

The editor or transcriber knows from context and/or observation how much text has been lost.

<gap> signifies text missing because of damage.

reason with value "lost" indicates that the text has been lost.

quantity indicates the amount of text has been lost.

unit indicates the unit measured in quantity.

‘[ ... ]’

<gap quantity="3" reason="lost"  unit="characters"/>

1.5. Editorial Deletion

Text suspected by the editor to be an interpolation.

<surplus> signifies text identified by the editor as added by someone other than the original author.

reason with value "interpolation" indicates why the text has been marked as surplus.

‘{lorem ipsum}’

<app>  <lem source="#Alonso">   <surplus reason="interpolation">lorem ipsum</surplus>  </lem> </app>

1.6. Deleted But Legible Text

Text deleted in an ancient source but still legible (more or less clearly).

<del> indicates the text that has been deleted but is still legible.

rend with value "erasure" indicates that the text has been erased. Other values: "strikethrough" and "expunction".

‘[[co]]gaudete’

<del rend="erasure">co</del>gaudete

1.7. Deleted and Lost

Text deleted in an ancient source to the point of illegibility.

<del> indicates that a deletion has occurred.

rend indicates the method of deletion (e.g., erasure, expunction, strikethrough).

<gap> indicates that the deletion has left a gap of irrecoverable text.

reason with value "lost" indicates that the text is irrecoverable.

quantity indicates the extent of the deletion.

unit indicates the unit of measurement for quantityt.

‘[[ ... ]]’

<del rend="erasure">  <gap quantity="3" unit="characters"/> </del>

1.8. Deleted and Illegible But Restored

Text deleted in an ancient source to the point of illegibility, but restored by the editor (e.g., by comparison with another exemplar).

<del> indicates that a deletion has occurred.

rend indicates the method of deletion (e.g., erasure, expunction).

<supplied> indicates the text that has been restored.

reason with value "lost" indicates why the text had to be supplied.

<orig> indicates that the supplied original cannot be understood.

<unclear> indicates that the supplied original is unclear.

‘[[ abc ]] ’

<app>  <lem>   <del rend="erasure">    <supplied reason="lost">abc</supplied> <!-- Use @resp to indicate who supplied the text? -->   </del>  </lem> </app>
<del rend="erasure">  <supplied reason="lost">   <orig>abc</orig>  </supplied> </del>
<del rend="erasure">  <supplied reason="lost">   <unclear>abc</unclear>  </supplied> </del>

1.9. Crux

Text deemed by the editor to be irredeemably corrupt.

<unclear> indicates that the text is readable but not understandable.

‘†lorem ipsum†’

<app>  <lem>   <unclear>lorem ipsum</unclear> <!-- How to indicate the this is an editor's determination? -->  </lem> </app>

2. Apparatus Criticus

This section provides models for handling the different kinds of data that occur in the apparatus criticus.

<app> (apparatus entry) contains the lemma and readings for a single entry in the apparatus criticus, though it and its contents may be nested inside of another <app>

<lem> (lemma) contains a reading judged by the editor to be what the author wrote, or as close as possible. This type of reading constitutes the text printed in the main portion of a critical edition.

<rdg> (reading) contains some text (or absence of text) witnessed by a manuscript or found in an early printed edition where it is not expressly proposed, but judged by the editor to be unsatisfactory or corrupt for linguistic and/or stylistic reasons. This text is printed in the apparatus criticus.

wit (witness) indicates any manuscript(s) or early printed edition(s) (esp. without an apparatus or commentary; i.e., editiones vetustiores)

source (source) indicates any printed edition(s) or other scholarly work(s) (e.g., article, textual note, commentary) addressing an aspect of the text.

ana (analysis) indicates that a reading belongs to at least one category in the LDLT's taxonomy of readings. More than one value is possible.

<witDetail> (witness detail) contains information about a particular witness (wit) to a lemma (<lem>) or reading (<rdg>).

<note> (note) contains editorial comments, bibliographical references, or other information pertaining to the apparatus entry.

2.2. Basic reporting of a variant reading

A witness or source has a reading that varies from the lemma in some way. For example, if the editor has accepted the text ‘Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet’ into the main text of the edition, but manuscript A has ‘larem’ instead of ‘lorem’, the variant reading would be reported in a printed edition as follows:

‘18 larem A’

Since the lemma is not typically printed in an apparatus criticus (unless there is a chance for confusion), it is up to the reader to recognize that ‘larem’ is a variant for ‘lorem’.

In this model, an <app> contains both the lemma and the variant reading, reducing the need for guesswork, at least when looking at the XML code.

The patterns displayed below are designed to handle the various kinds of information conveyed in a typical apparatus criticus, including readings ante correctionem and in rasura, among other paleographical and editorial details.

<p>  <app>   <lem>Lorem</lem>   <rdg wit="#A">larem</rdg>  </app> ipsum dolor sit amet </p>

If an editor deems it important to indicate that a <rdg> also appears in a source, the same pattern applies, but source is added after wit:

<p>  <app>   <lem>Lorem</lem>   <rdg source="#Jones" wit="#A">larem</rdg>  </app> ipsum dolor sit amet </p>

If there are multiple variant readings, another <rdg> should be added for each one.

<p>  <app>   <lem>Lorem</lem>   <rdg source="#Jones" wit="#A">larem</rdg>   <rdg wit="#B">lurum</rdg>  </app> ipsum dolor sit amet </p>

2.3. Additions

A witness or source adds text (e.g., in the margin) that an editor deems worthy of notice.

<add> (addition) contains text that has been added.

place indicates the placed where the text has been added. Possible values:

  • above: above the line (suprascr. = superscriptum/a)
  • below: below the line (subscr. = subscriptum/a)
  • bottom: bottom of the page (ad fin. = ad finem
  • inline: within the body of the original text (in textu)
  • inRas: in the space where text has been erased. (in ras. = in rasura)
  • margin: right, left, or both (in mg. = in margine)
  • top: at the top of the page (ad init. = ad initium

hand indicates the manuscript hand that added the text. See the section on manuscript hands for more information about encoding that information.

For example, the copyist of manuscript N has the lemma, but a second hand has added a variant reading in the margin.

‘89 certare] certate G in mg.’

<app>  <lem>certare</lem>  <rdg wit="#G" xml:id="rdg89certate">   <add hand="#G1" place="margin">certate</add>  </rdg> </app>

2.4. Deletions

A witness or source has deleted some text, and an editor deems it worthy of notice.

<del> (deletion) contains text that has been deleted.

rend (rendition) indicates the method of deletion. Possible values:

  • erasure: text has been physically removed from the page or lines have been drawn through it (del. = delevit/deleverunt)
  • expunction: text has been marked for deletion with dots underneath (exp. = expunxit/expunxerunt)

For example, the copyist of manuscript N has printed the lemma tristique, but a later hand has deleted the letters que.

‘tristi (que del. N²)’

<app ana="#subtractive">  <lem>tristique</lem>  <rdg wit="#N">tristi<del hand="#N2" rend="erasure">que</del>  </rdg> </app>

Note that ana with value "subtractive" is on the <app> to indicate that the entire entry concerns a reading that takes away something from the lemma. If there are several <rdg> elements and not all of them are subtractive in nature, ana would be applied to the relevant <rdg>.

2.5. Omissions

A witness or source omits some text and the editor deems it important to report the omission.

For example, the witness known as "P" has omitted the word pecus from the following text: securo custode pecus nocturnaque pastor. The traditional apparatus criticus indicates the omission as follows:

‘pecus om. P’

In this model, the omission is represented by a "self-closing" <rdg>:

<l>securo custode <app ana="#subtractive">   <lem>pecus</lem>   <rdg wit="#P"/>  </app> nocturnaque pastor</l>

Note that ana is used with the value "subtractive" to enable querying for this type of data. If there is only one <rdg>, then ana="subtractive" is attached to <app> to indicate that the entire entry in the apparatus concerns an omission. If an omission is to be reported among other readings, ana="subtractive" is applied to the <rdg> that reports the omission:

<l>securo custode <app>   <lem>pecus</lem>   <rdg wit="#B">pecore</rdg>   <rdg ana="#subtractive" wit="#P"/>  </app> nocturnaque pastor</l>

The same model applies to the omission of an entire line of verse, but in that case <app> (with type="lineOmission") and <lem> contain the entire line, and the omission is marked by a self-closing <rdg>:

<app type="lineOmission">  <lem>   <l n="3">et spument rauco ferventia musta susurro.</l>  </lem>  <rdg wit="#κ #χ"/> </app>

This is displayed in the apparatus criticus as ‘vers. om. κχ’

2.6. Lacuna

A witness is missing a portion of the text.

<lacunaStart> is a self-closing tag with wit indicating where in a witness the lacuna begins.

<lacunaEnd> is a self-closing tag with wit indicating where in a witness the lacuna ends.

For example, witness N has a lacuna that begins after the word metus and ends before the word arcu in the following passage: ‘Mauris ut mauris et metus pharetra dictum et risus. Suspendisse ut maximus diam. Suspendisse luctus diam non libero pellentesque, et aliquam ex congue. Suspendisse vitae lacinia urna, nec egestas arcu. Quisque eget.’

A traditional apparatus criticus might have the following to indicate the lacuna:

‘pharetra … arcu deest N’

<p>Mauris ut mauris et metus <app>   <lem>pharetra</lem>   <rdg wit="#N">    <lacunaStart/>   </rdg>  </app> dictum et risus. Suspendisse ut maximus diam. Suspendisse luctus diam non libero pellentesque, et aliquam ex congue. Suspendisse vitae lacinia urna, nec <app>   <lem>egestas</lem>   <rdg wit="#N">    <lacunaEnd/>   </rdg>  </app> arcu. Quisque eget.</p>

2.7. Corrections

A variant reading has been corrected to the value of the lemma, but the editor deems the reading prior to correction worthy of notice.

<rdgGrp> (reading group) contains the readings before and after correction, but since the emphasis is on the original, uncorrected, reading, type is applied with the value "anteCorr" (i.e., ante correctionem)

varSeq (variant sequence) indicates the order of the readings (e.g., 1 for the first reading, 2 for the correction.

copyOf indicates that the correction is a copy of the lemma. Instead of copying the value of the lemma, we point to it to indicate that they are the same.

For example, manuscript N originally had quaedam for the lemma quidem. The editor wishes to note the reading ante correctionem.

In a traditional critical edition, this would be handled in the apparatus criticus as follows: ‘quaedam N (ante corr. N¹).’ There is no need to print the result of the correction, since "correction" implies the editor's accepted reading (i.e., lemma).

<app>  <lem xml:id="lem1.33quidem">quidem</lem>  <rdgGrp type="anteCorr">   <rdg varSeq="1" wit="#N">quaedam</rdg>   <rdg copyOf="#lem1.33quidem" varSeq="2"    wit="#N1"/>  </rdgGrp> </app>

Note that the <lem> must have an xml:id to be a target for copyOf

This pattern is the basis for a number of other representations of reading sequences.

2.7.1. Correction in the Margin

A manuscript hand has corrected a variant reading to the lemma by writing it in the margin.

For example, manuscript G has iantabimur in the text, but a second hand has added the accepted reading mactabimus in the margin. This would be handled in a traditional apparatus as follows: ‘iantabimur G (corr. G² in mg.)’.

<app>  <lem xml:id="lem4.166mactabimus">mactabimus</lem>  <rdgGrp type="anteCorr">   <rdg varSeq="1" wit="#G">iantabimur</rdg>   <rdg varSeq="2" wit="#G">    <add copyOf="#lem4.166mactabimus"     place="margin"/>   </rdg>  </rdgGrp> </app>

A self-closing <add> is used here with place and copyOf to indicate that the marginal reading is identical to the accepted reading.

2.7.2. Correction of an Omission

The original copyist has omitted a reading from the text, but another hand (or the original copyist) has supplied the missing text.

For example, manuscript G has omitted the lemma tandem, but the original copyist has supplied it. In a traditional critical edition, that would be represented as follows: ‘tandem] om. G (corr. G¹)’.

<app>  <lem xml:id="lem1.43tandem">tandem</lem>  <rdgGrp type="corrOmission">   <rdg varSeq="1" wit="#G"/>   <rdg copyOf="#lem1.43tandem" varSeq="2"    wit="#G1"/>  </rdgGrp> </app>

If the corrector has added the reading in the margin or elsewhere (e.g., ‘tandem] om. G (corr. G¹ in mg.)’, <add> with place and copyOf can be used:

<app ana="#subtractive">  <lem xml:id="lem1.43tandem">tandem</lem>  <rdgGrp type="corrOmission">   <rdg varSeq="1" wit="#G"/>   <rdg varSeq="2" wit="#G">    <add copyOf="#lem1.43tandem"     place="margin"/>   </rdg>  </rdgGrp> </app>

2.7.3. Correction by Addition

A variant reading differs from the lemma by one or more characters, which have been supplied by the original copyist or another hand at a later time.

For example, manuscript N has insidia, which differs from the lemma insidiaby a single letter "s". A later hand has added the missing "s". That might be handled as follows in a traditional apparatus criticus: insidia N (s add. N²).

<app>  <lem xml:id="lem3.2insidias">insidias</lem>  <rdgGrp type="anteCorr">   <rdg varSeq="1" wit="#N">insidia</rdg>   <rdg varSeq="2" wit="#N2">insidia<add place="inline">s</add>   </rdg> <!-- Or should this be handled by a <witDetail>? Or both? -->  </rdgGrp> </app>

2.7.4. Correction by Deletion

A variant reading differs from the lemma by one or more characters, which have been deleted by the original copyist or another hand at a later time.

For example, manuscript N has the reading cogaudete where the lemma is just gaudete. The original copyist has deleted the letters "co" from the beginning of the word. In a traditional apparatus criticus, that might be printed as follows: ‘cogaudete N (corr. N¹, co exp.)’.

<app>  <lem xml:id="lem2.5gaudete">gaudete</lem>  <rdgGrp type="anteCorr">   <rdg varSeq="1" wit="#N">cogaudete</rdg>   <rdg varSeq="2" wit="#N1"    xml:id="rdg2.5gaudete">    <del rend="expunction">co</del>gaudete</rdg>  </rdgGrp> </app>

2.7.5. One Witness Among Many Has Been Corrected

A variant reading has several witnesses, but only one of them has been corrected, and the editor believes it is important to make that clear.

For example, manuscripts P Q R S T U V have the variant reading flore where the lemma is Flora. Only manuscript T has been corrected. In a traditional apparatus criticus, that might be handled as follows: ‘flore PQRST (corr. T¹) UV’.

Since only one witness has been corrected, <rdgGrp> with type="anteCorr" cannot contain the reading of all of the manuscripts, so we must use copyOf on both the lemma and the variant reading.

<app>  <lem xml:id="lem1.2Flora">Flora</lem>  <rdg wit="#P #Q #R #S #U #V"   xml:id="rdg1.2">flore</rdg>  <rdgGrp>   <rdg copyOf="#rdg1.2flore" varSeq="1"    wit="#T"/>   <rdg copyOf="#lem1.2Flora" varSeq="2"    wit="#T1"/>  </rdgGrp> <!-- Need to consider whether this should be done with <witDetail> instead or in addition to it. --> </app>

2.7.6. Mutation

Sometimes the copyist or a later hand "corrects" a variant reading to another variant reading. Although the motivation may have been to correct, the action results in mutation.

For example, manuscript N has dipicta for lemma descripta, and the original copyist has "corrected" the reading to depicta.

A traditional apparatus criticus might handle that as follows: ‘dipicta N (mut. in depicta N¹)’.

The same pattern for the ante correctionem scenarios can be used here, with a different value for type ("mutation") on <rdgGrp>:

<app>  <lem wit="#P #V #N2">descripta</lem>  <rdgGrp type="mutation">   <rdg varSeq="1" wit="#N">dipicta</rdg>   <rdg varSeq="2" wit="#N1">depicta</rdg>  </rdgGrp> </app>

2.8. Conjecture

A conjecture is some portion of text (or absence of text) proposed by some person (ancient or modern) and determined by an editor to be worthy of consideration in establishing the text, but not to be printed in the main text of the edition.

In a traditional critical edition, a conjecture might be noted as follows: ‘rapidoque] rabidoque conj. Baehrens’.

Although conjectures are more common in modern editions and other scholarship, they are not unheard of in manuscripts, where a hand has written a reading (e.g., in the margin) that does not resemble any other variant readings and cannot be attributed to a recognizable error. Therefore, conjecture is not limited to those materials identified in source. For this reason, type with value "conjecture" must be used to identify a reading as a conjecture.

<app type="conjecture">  <lem>rapidoque</lem>  <rdg source="#Baehrens">rabidoque</rdg> </app>

In this example, type is attached to <app> because the entire entry concerns a conjecture. If the conjecture is just one among many readings, type must be used on the individual <rdg> that contains the conjecture.

<app>  <lem>bullantes</lem>  <rdg wit="#ε #δ #e #d2">bullentes</rdg>  <rdg wit="#η #ν #φ #ρ2 #r #s">bullantis</rdg>  <rdg source="#Heins." type="#conjecture">palantes</rdg> </app>

If a single scholar has proposed multiple conjectures for a single lemma, <rdgGrp> with type contains all of the conjectures as individual readings:

<app>  <lem>nullos ducet</lem>  <rdgGrp type="conjecture">   <rdg source="#Heins.">nullosque sui</rdg>   <rdg source="#Heins.">nullosque ducis</rdg>   <rdg source="#Heins.">nullosque ducum</rdg>  </rdgGrp> </app>

That should be displayed as: ‘nullosque sui vel nullosque duics vel nullosque ducum conj. Heins.’

2.9. Emendation

An emendation is a "successful" conjecture. That is, an emendation is some portion of text (or absence of text) expressly proposed by some person through the application of critical judgment, accepted by the editor in place of the transmitted readings, and printed in the main text of an edition. For this reason, only a lemma can be classified as an emendation.

The following example illustrates the difference between an emendation and a conjecture by virtue of the fact that the <lem> has type="emendation" and <rdg> has type="conjecture":

<app>  <lem source="#Ulit." type="emendation">niteat</lem>  <rdg wit="#N #G #β #κ #π #ρ1 #λ1">mutat</rdg>  <rdg source="#p #t #h #Kempfer #Burm."   wit="#P #δ">mittat</rdg>  <rdg ana="#lexical" wit="#μ">micat</rdg>  <rdg source="#o #n #l2 #i2 #Guid. #Wernsd. #Beck #Glaeser #Keene"   wit="#θ #ν #α #η #r #s">nutet</rdg>  <rdg source="#i1 #l1 #Tig. #Vienn."   wit="#ε #χ #γ #ρ2 #λ2 #d #e #u #b">nutat</rdg>  <rdg wit="#φ">mutet</rdg>  <rdg source="#Leo" type="conjecture">micet ac</rdg>  <rdg source="#Barth" type="conjecture">nictat</rdg> </app>

2.10. Notes

An editor wishes to comment on a reading, include bibliographical information, or note something else about the passage in question.

For example, the editor has recorded a variant reading that another scholar has supported in a textual note. That would be handled in a traditional edition as follows: molle sub] molliter Glaeser, def. Jacoby (Wochenschrift für klassische Philologie III, 1886, p. 1290).

We handle this with <note> with target pointing at the value of xml:id on the <lem> or <rdg> in question.

<app>  <lem source="#edd." wit="#P #V">molle sub</lem>  <rdg source="#Glaeser #Keene"   wit="#N #G #A #H" xml:id="rdg1.5molliter">molliter</rdg>  <rdg source="#Schenkl2">molle per</rdg>  <note target="#rdg1.5molliter">   <ref target="#Jacoby">Jacoby</ref> defendit (<ref target="https://books.google.de/books?id=q5blAAAAMAAJ&amp;hl=de&amp;pg=PA128#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false">Wochenschrift für klassische Philologie</ref> III, 1886, p. 1290)</note> </app>
